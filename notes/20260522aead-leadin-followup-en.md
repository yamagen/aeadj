# Lead-in Chain and Follow-up Chain

Last change: 2026/05/22-17:02:14.

Hilofumi Yamamoto, Institute of Science Tokyo

## Introduction

In language use, there are sometimes chains placed before or after a main expression. These chains support the activation of the main expression, or adjust its meaning, attitude, responsibility, and interpersonal distance after it has been uttered. For example, in an utterance such as "I did not hear it directly, but apparently that shop is closing," the expression "apparently" alone does not create the full assessment. The preceding expression "I did not hear it directly, but" allows the following statement "that shop is closing" to be uttered in a stable way.

A chain placed before the main expression that stabilizes the following utterance, response, or judgment is called a lead-in chain. In Japanese, it is called 前置連鎖.

A chain placed after the main expression that adjusts the assertiveness, responsibility, emotion, interpersonal distance, or related aspects of the already uttered content is called a follow-up chain. In Japanese, it is called 後置連鎖.

A lead-in chain is not a prefix, and a follow-up chain is not a suffix. These are not morphemes directly attached to words. Rather, they are utterance-level units placed before or after the whole expression. They either help the following chain arise, or adjust a chain that has already arisen.

Example:

- I did not hear it directly, but apparently that shop is closing.

In this case, "I did not hear it directly, but" is the lead-in chain, and "apparently that shop is closing" is the main expression. The lead-in chain allows the speaker to set up the certainty of the information and their own stance before making the assessment with "apparently."

Example:

- That shop is closing, apparently. I did not hear it directly, though.

In this case, "I did not hear it directly, though" functions as a follow-up chain. It adjusts the assertiveness and responsibility of the already uttered assessment.

Thus, lead-in chains and follow-up chains are important chain units in Immediate Grammar. They support the activation of the main expression and adjust the meaning, attitude, or responsibility of the expression after it has been activated.

## From a Conventional View to a New View

In conventional accounts, expressions placed before the main expression, such as "I did not hear it directly, but," "I am not really sure, but," or "somehow," are often treated as hedges, markers of evidentiality, or explanations of the information source. Expressions placed after the main expression, such as "I do not know for sure," "I did not hear it directly, though," or "probably," are often treated as supplementary information or additional comments.

However, from the perspective of Immediate Grammar, which treats language use as a process unfolding in time, these elements are not merely peripheral supplements. A lead-in chain prepares the speaker's attitude, distance, hesitation, non-commitment, or handling of information before the main expression is uttered, and thereby stabilizes the activation of the following main expression. A follow-up chain, in contrast, adjusts the meaning, attitude, responsibility, or interpersonal distance of the already uttered main expression.

In this sense, lead-in chains and follow-up chains are not matters of morphological attachment or fixed phrase structure. They are operational units on the utterance chain. They are selected in real time to support the activation of the main expression or to adjust how an already activated expression is received.

Therefore, lead-in chains and follow-up chains should not be treated as merely pragmatic add-ons. They have a formal role in utterance formation. Lead-in chains support the activation of the main expression, while follow-up chains adjust the main expression after it has been uttered. This is why they are central to the description of chain activation in Immediate Grammar.

## Definitions

Lead-in chains and follow-up chains can both be immediate expressions. However, their functions differ according to their position. A lead-in chain placed before the main expression functions as support for the main expression, helping the following expression to be activated and uttered stably. A follow-up chain placed after the main expression functions as adjustment of the main expression, adjusting the meaning, attitude, and responsibility of an expression that has already been activated.

In this sense, a follow-up chain is uttered in real time, but its function is close to Adjustive Grammar. This is where Immediate Grammar and Adjustive Grammar meet. Lead-in chains work toward making the main expression activatable, whereas follow-up chains work toward readjusting a main expression that has already been activated.

### Lead-in Chain

A lead-in chain is a chain of support for the main expression. It is placed before the main expression and allows the speaker to set up their attitude, distance, hesitation, non-commitment, or handling of information, thereby helping the following main expression to be activated and uttered stably.

### Follow-up Chain

A follow-up chain is a chain of adjustment for the main expression. It is placed after the main expression and adjusts the meaning, attitude, or responsibility of already uttered content. After uttering the main expression, the speaker can soften, strengthen, or redirect its assertiveness or responsibility.

## Examples

### Lead-in Chain

- Example 1: I did not hear it directly, but apparently that shop is closing.
- Example 2: Somehow, just from the look of it, they seem to be feeling unwell.
- Example 3: I am not really sure, but that person seems a little unusual.

### Follow-up Chain

- Example 1: That shop is closing, apparently. I did not hear it directly, though.
- Example 2: They seem to be feeling unwell, somehow, just from the look of it.
- Example 3: That person seems a little unusual, I do not know for sure, though.

## Not Limited to Japanese

Lead-in chains and follow-up chains are not phenomena limited to Japanese. Similar chains can be found in English, French, Spanish, German, Chinese, and other languages. They are placed before or after the main expression and support the activation of the utterance or adjust it afterward.

These units are neither affixes directly attached to words nor merely syntactic structures. A lead-in chain placed before the main expression prepares the speaker's attitude, distance, non-commitment, or handling of information, thereby making the following main expression easier to utter stably. A follow-up chain placed after the main expression adjusts the assertiveness, responsibility, or attitude of what has already been uttered.

The following examples use roughly the same content, corresponding to "apparently that shop is closing."

### Lead-in Chain

- Japanese: 直接聞いたわけじゃないんだけど、あの店、閉店するみたい。
- English: I'm not sure, but apparently that shop is closing.
- French: Je ne suis pas sûr, mais apparemment ce magasin va fermer.
- Spanish: No estoy seguro, pero parece que esa tienda va a cerrar.
- German: Ich bin mir nicht sicher, aber anscheinend macht der Laden zu.
- Chinese: 我不是直接听说的，不过那家店好像要关门了。

### Follow-up Chain

- Japanese: あの店、閉店するみたい。直接聞いたわけじゃないんだけど。
- English: That shop is closing, apparently. I'm not sure, though.
- French: Ce magasin va fermer, apparemment. Je ne suis pas sûr cependant.
- Spanish: Esa tienda va a cerrar, por lo visto. No lo he oído directamente.
- German: Der Laden macht wohl zu. Ich bin mir nicht sicher.
- Chinese: 那家店好像要关门了。我不是直接听说的。

## Similar Concepts

Lead-in chains and follow-up chains are concepts concerned with position in the utterance flow, and may seem similar to prefix / suffix or preposition / postposition. However, the unit involved is different. Prefixes and suffixes are elements directly attached to words or morphemes. Prepositions and postpositions are placed before or after words and form phrases.

In contrast, lead-in chains and follow-up chains are not directly attached to words or phrases. They are utterance-level units placed before or after an utterance or judgment. They stabilize the following chain, or adjust the meaning, attitude, responsibility, or distance of a chain that has already been uttered.

Therefore, the terms "lead-in" and "follow-up" do not refer to morphological attachment or syntactic position inside a phrase. They refer to position and function in the utterance chain.

## Effects

The effect of a lead-in chain lies in preparing the speaker's stance and the ground for the utterance before the main expression is produced. If the main expression is uttered abruptly by itself, it may sound too assertive, insufficiently grounded, or too sudden for the listener. By placing a lead-in chain such as "I did not hear it directly, but," "I am not really sure, but," or "somehow," the speaker can first signal attitude, distance, hesitation, non-commitment, or information handling. As a result, the following main expression can arise more stably than when it is uttered alone.

For example, it is possible to say simply, "That shop is closing, apparently." However, when the speaker says, "I did not hear it directly, but apparently that shop is closing," the speaker first shows that the information has not been directly confirmed and then shares an assessment. Here, the lead-in chain is not merely explaining the source of information. It enables the following expression "that shop is closing" to be uttered not as a firm assertion, but as an assessment from some distance.

The effect of a follow-up chain lies in adjusting how the utterance will be received after the main expression has already been uttered. After producing the main expression, the speaker can add a follow-up chain such as "I did not hear it directly, though," "I do not know for sure," "probably," or "well, that is just how it looks" in order to adjust the assertiveness, responsibility, attitude, or interpersonal distance of what has already been said.

For example, when the speaker says, "That shop is closing, apparently. I did not hear it directly, though," the speaker first presents an assessment and then adjusts the certainty of the information or the scope of responsibility for the utterance. This is not simply supplementary information. It is an operation that resets the strength of meaning or the range of responsibility after the utterance has already arisen. Therefore, a follow-up chain is not extra information added after the main expression, but a mechanism for readjusting an already activated chain.

A lead-in chain stabilizes the activation of the main expression. A follow-up chain adjusts the main expression after it has been activated. Together, these two operations make utterances safer, more natural, and more acceptable in interpersonal interaction.

## Previous Studies

Lead-in chains and follow-up chains have points of contact with research on discourse markers, hedges, evidentiality, stance expressions, and pre-sequences in conversation analysis.

Regarding discourse markers, Schiffrin (1987) argues that discourse markers provide coordinates for interpretation in the local and global organization of conversation. Fraser (1999) treats discourse markers as lexical expressions that signal relations between discourse segments. Lead-in chains and follow-up chains also adjust relations between utterances. However, they differ in that they are treated not only as single words or short phrases, but as chain units that may include longer phrases or clauses.

Regarding hedges, Lakoff (1973) discusses hedges in relation to fuzzy concepts. Lead-in chains such as "I am not really sure, but" or "I did not hear it directly, but" are related to hedges in that they weaken assertion. However, in the present account, a lead-in chain is not limited to weakening assertion. It also supports the stable activation of the following main expression.

Regarding evidentiality, Aikhenvald (2004) describes evidentiality as a grammatical category that marks information source or type of evidence. Lead-in chains such as "I did not hear it directly, but" or "I did not see it myself, but" are related to evidentiality because they concern information source and degree of certainty. However, lead-in chains are not limited to marking evidence. They may also involve hesitation, distance, interpersonal consideration, and avoidance of assertion.

Regarding stance, Du Bois (2007) presents the stance triangle and treats stance as an interactional phenomenon involving evaluation, positioning, and alignment with others. Lead-in chains and follow-up chains are related to stance formation because they allow speakers to adjust their position, distance from the listener, and responsibility for the utterance before or after the main expression.

In conversation analysis, Schegloff's (1980) discussion of pre-sequences and preliminaries is important. An utterance such as "Can I ask you a question?" can secure the possibility of acceptance before the main action is performed. A lead-in chain is related to this in that it prepares the ground before entering the main expression. However, in this account, lead-in chains are treated more broadly, not only as preparations for questions or requests, but also as chains involved in assessment, non-commitment, emotional expression, and relationship adjustment.

Thus, lead-in chains and follow-up chains overlap with discourse markers, hedges, evidentiality, stance, and pre-sequences, but they cannot be reduced to any one of these categories. They are positioned here as operational units in Immediate Grammar. They are placed before or after the main expression to stabilize the following chain or to adjust the meaning, attitude, or responsibility of an utterance after it has arisen.

In this respect, lead-in chains and follow-up chains are not merely types of discourse marker or hedge. They are operational units for describing chain activation potential in Immediate Grammar.

## Conclusion

This note defined lead-in chains and follow-up chains and presented examples. These chains play an important role in Immediate Grammar by supporting the activation of the main expression and by adjusting its meaning, attitude, and responsibility after it has been uttered.
