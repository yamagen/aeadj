No doubt about it

間違まちがいない

Machigai nai

No doubt about it

Variants

  • 間違まちがいない
    No doubt about it
  • 間違まちがいないって
    I'm telling you
  • 間違まちがいないよ
    I'm sure of it
  • 間違まちがいないから
    Definitely
  • 絶対ぜったい、そうだよ
    I'm certain

Adjusted Expression

A「明日あしたは晴はれるでしょうか」B「はい、晴はれる可能性かのうせいが高たかいと思おもいます」

A: Do you think it will be sunny tomorrow? B: Yes, I think it is very likely to be sunny.

Example

A「明日あしたは晴はれるかな？」B「間違まちがいないよ」

A: Do you think it will be sunny tomorrow? B: No doubt about it.

Notes

"Machigai nai" expresses strong confidence in the moment. It can be used not only for facts that are certain, but also for things that cannot be fully guaranteed, such as the weather. In this example, it gives a firm answer to A's uncertain question.

I have to eat

食たべなくちゃ

Tabenakucha

I have to eat

Variants

  • 食たべなくちゃ
    I have to eat
  • 食たべなきゃ
    I need to eat
  • 食たべないと
    I gotta eat
  • 食たべなきゃいけない
    I should eat
  • 食たべなければなりません
    I must eat

Adjusted Expression

A「そろそろ食事しょくじをしなければなりません」B「そうですね。食たべたほうがいいですね」

A: I should eat soon. B: Yes, you should eat.

Example

A「もう12時じだ。食たべなくちゃ」B「うん、食たべなきゃ」

A: It's already twelve. I have to eat. B: Yeah, you should.

Notes

"Tabenakucha" is a casual shortened form of "tabenakereba ikenai," meaning "I have to eat." In this example, the speaker notices the time and prompts themself to act. Rather than a formal obligation, it works as a short self-prompt that moves the speaker toward the next action.

    It's so good, though...
  • おいしかったのに

In this example, A says they do not like natto, and B responds with "oishii noni..." in a disappointed way. By stopping at "noni," the speaker leaves feelings such as "Why don't you like it?" or "What a waste" unstated.
