2026.06.20 Sat / No. 801 / konnakotonante

This has never happened before.

こんなことなんて今いままでなかったのに。

Konna koto nante ima made nakatta noni.

This has never happened before.

Variants

  • こんなこと、今いままでなかったのに。
    This has never happened before.
  • こんなこと、今いままで起おきたことがなかったのに。
    This kind of thing has never happened before.
  • こんなこと、今いままで経験けいけんしたことがなかったのに。
    I have never experienced anything like this before.

Adjusted Expression

今いままで、こんなことは起おきたことがなかったのに。

Until now, this kind of thing had never happened.

Example

A「バスばす、こないねぇ」 B「そうねぇ、こんなことなんて今いままでなかったのに」

A: The bus isn't coming, huh. B: Yeah, this has never happened before.

Notes

'Konna koto nante ima made nakatta noni.' is used to express surprise or confusion about something that has never happened before. 'Nante' makes it more than a factual statement; it gives the feeling of 'I never expected something like this to happen.'

2026.06.21 Sun / No. 802 / suyasuya

Sleeping peacefully.

すやすやと眠ねむっている。

Suyasuya to nemutte iru.

Sleeping peacefully.

Variants

  • すやすやと眠ねむっている。
    Sleeping peacefully.
  • すやすや眠ねむっている。
    Sleeping soundly.
  • すやすやと寝ねている。
    Sound asleep.

Adjusted Expression

すやすやと眠ねむっています。

They are sleeping peacefully.

Example

A「赤あかちゃん、眠ねむってる？」 B「いや、お父とうさんの方ほうが、すやすやと眠ねむってるけど」

A: Is the baby asleep? B: No, actually, Dad is the one sleeping peacefully.

Notes

'Suyasuya' is an onomatopoeic expression for sleeping quietly and peacefully. It is often used for babies or children, but it can also describe an adult sleeping comfortably. In the example, the funny point is that it is not the baby but the father who is sleeping peacefully.

2026.06.22 Mon / No. 803 / sonnakotoba

Where did you learn that word?

そんなことば、どこで覚おぼえたの？

Sonna kotoba, doko de oboeta no?

Where did you learn that word?

Variants

  • そんなことば、どこで覚おぼえたの？
    Where did you learn that word?
  • そんなことば、どこで聞きいたの？
    Where did you hear that word?
  • そんなことば、どこで知しったの？
    Where did you come across that word?

Adjusted Expression

そのようなことばを、どこで覚おぼえたのですか？

Where did you learn such a word?

Example

A「パパぱぱ、それ、コンプラ的こんぷらてきにアウトあうとじゃない？」 B「レイれいちゃん、そんなことば、どこで覚おぼえたの？」

A: Dad, isn't that kind of a compliance issue? B: Rei-chan, where did you learn that phrase?

Notes

'Sonna kotoba, doko de oboeta no?' is used when someone uses a word or phrase that feels unexpected for them, such as a very grown-up expression from a child. 'Sonna kotoba' points not only to the word itself, but also to the surprise that this person used it. In the example, Rei-chan says 'konpura-teki ni auto,' a very adult-sounding phrase, so B reacts with surprise.

2026.06.23 Tue / No. 804 / yokumiminisurune

I hear that a lot.

よく耳みみにするね。

Yoku mimi ni suru ne.

I hear that a lot.

Variants

  • よく耳みみにするね。
    I hear that a lot.
  • よく聞きくね。
    I often hear that.
  • よく耳みみにする話はなしだね。
    That's something I hear often.

Adjusted Expression

よく耳みみにしますね。

I often hear that.

Example

最近さいきん、よく耳みみにするね、この話はなし。

I've been hearing about this a lot lately.

Notes

'Yoku mimi ni suru ne' is used when a topic or piece of information comes up often around you. Compared with 'kiku' (hear/listen), 'mimi ni suru' sounds less intentional; it suggests that the information naturally reaches your ears. The final 'ne' adds the feeling that the speaker expects the listener to recognize the same situation.

2026.06.24 Wed / No. 805 / tsukamaranaine

It's hard to get a taxi, isn't it?

なかなか掴つかまらないね、タクシーたくしー。

Nakanaka tsukamaranai ne, takushii.

It's hard to get a taxi, isn't it?

Variants

  • なかなか掴つかまらないね、タクシーたくしー。
    It's hard to get a taxi, isn't it?
  • なかなか捕つかまらないね、タクシーたくしー。
    It's difficult to get a taxi, isn't it?
  • なかなか拾ひろえないね、タクシーたくしー。
    It's not easy to hail a taxi, is it?

Adjusted Expression

タクシーたくしーがなかなか掴つかまりませんね。

It's quite difficult to get a taxi, isn't it?

Example

A「なかなか掴つかまらないね、タクシーたくしー」 B「駅前えきまえまで歩あるくしかないね」

A: It's hard to get a taxi, isn't it? B: No choice but to walk to the station, then.

Notes

'Nakanaka tsukamaranai ne, takushii' is used when you are having trouble finding or hailing a taxi. Here, 'tsukamaru' does not mean physically grabbing the taxi with your hand; it gives the metaphorical sense of getting hold of something you can use. 'Nakanaka' shows that something is not happening even though you are expecting it to happen. By placing 'takushii' at the end, the speaker first expresses the difficulty and then adds the topic in a natural spoken style. In the example, 'aruku shika nai ne' is rendered as 'No choice but to walk,' a short reaction-like phrase showing that walking is the only realistic option left.

2026.06.25 Thu / No. 806 / aakorekore

Ah, this is it.

ああ、これこれ。

Aa, kore kore.

Ah, this is it.

Variants

  • ああ、これこれ。
    Ah, this is it.
  • あ、これこれ。
    Ah, this is the one.
  • ああ、これだよ。
    Ah, this is what I was looking for.

Adjusted Expression

ああ、これですね。

Ah, this is the one.

Example

A「ねぇ、これじゃない？ ほしかったの」 B「ああ、これこれ」

A: Hey, isn't this the one you wanted? B: Ah, this is it.

Notes

'Aa, kore kore' is used when you find something just as expected, or when something brings back a familiar memory. It expresses satisfaction and recognition. For example, it can be used when you find a nostalgic taste, discover something you have been looking for for a long time, or encounter a situation that feels just right. Even if the thing is far away, or even if the other person is holding it, the speaker says 'kore kore,' not 'are are' or 'sore sore,' to express the joy of finding exactly what they expected.

2026.06.26 Fri / No. 807 / yuzuttara

Why don't you share a little?

ちょっと、譲ゆずったら？

Chotto, yuzuttara?

Why don't you share a little?

Variants

  • ちょっと、譲ゆずったら？
    Why don't you share a little?
  • 少すこし、譲ゆずってみたら？
    Why don't you let them have some?
  • 少すこし、譲ゆずってみてはどう？
    How about giving them a little?

Adjusted Expression

それぐらいは、譲ゆずってみてはいかがですか。

Why don't you let them have at least that much?

Example

A「たくさん持もっているんだから、ちょっと、譲ゆずったら？」 B「え、だって」

A: You have a lot, so why don't you share a little? B: But...

Notes

'Chotto, yuzuttara?' is used to suggest that someone should share a little or let someone else have some, especially when they have a lot or are holding on to their share. Here, 'yuzuru' means giving up a small part of what one has, or giving way a little. 'Chotto' makes the suggestion sound lighter than a direct command, but depending on the situation, it can also carry a mild note of reproach.

2026.06.27 Sat / No. 808 / yadayo

No way, not that.

やだよ、そんなの。

Yada yo, sonna no.

No way, not that.

Variants

  • やだよ、そんなの。
    No way, not that.
  • いやだよ、そんなの。
    No, I don't want to.
  • やだなぁ、そんなの。
    Ugh, I don't like that.

Adjusted Expression

そういうのは嫌いやです。

I don't like that kind of thing.

Example

A「たまには、こんな色いろのを着きてみたら？」 B「やだよ、そんなの」

A: Why don't you try wearing this color for a change? B: No way, not that.

Notes

'Yada yo, sonna no' is a casual spoken reaction used to reject a suggestion or something in front of you. 'Yada' is a shortened, casual form of 'iya da,' and it gives the refusal before any explanation. 'Sonna no' distances the speaker from the suggestion a little, as if saying 'that kind of thing.' It sounds natural among friends or family, but depending on the person and situation, it can also sound strong or childish.

2026.06.28 Sun / No. 809 / iikini

I got carried away and messed up.

いい気きになって、失敗しっぱいしちゃった。

Ii ki ni natte, shippai shichatta.

I got carried away and messed up.

Variants

  • いい気きになって、失敗しっぱいしちゃった。
    I got carried away and messed up.
  • 調子ちょうしにのって、失敗しっぱいしちゃった。
    I got cocky and messed up.
  • 自信過剰じしんかじょうで、失敗しっぱいしちゃった。
    I was overconfident and messed up.

Adjusted Expression

調子ちょうしに乗のってしまい、失敗しっぱいしてしまいました。

I got carried away and ended up making a mistake.

Example

A「うまくいってたのにね」 B「うん、いい気きになって、失敗しっぱいしちゃった」

A: Things were going well, too. B: Yeah, I got carried away and messed up.

Notes

'Ii ki ni natte, shippai shichatta' is a casual spoken expression used when the speaker says they got carried away, became a little too confident, and ended up making a mistake. Here, 'ii ki ni naru' does not simply mean feeling good; it suggests getting a bit carried away or letting success go to your head. 'Shichatta' is the casual form of 'shite shimatta,' and it adds a sense of regret or disappointment about what happened.

2026.06.29 Mon / No. 810 / yacchaimashitane

I messed up, didn't I?

やっちゃいましたね。

Yacchaimashita ne.

I messed up, didn't I?

Variants

  • やっちゃいましたね。
    I messed up, didn't I?
  • やってしまいましたね。
    I really messed up, didn't I?
  • やっちゃったね。
    You messed up, huh.

Adjusted Expression

やってしまいましたね。

I really made a mistake, didn't I?

Example

A「締切しめきり、まだ間まに合あうよね」 B「え？ 昨日きのうでしたよ」 A「ああ、やっちゃいましたね」

A: The deadline is still okay, right? B: Huh? It was yesterday. A: Oh, I messed up, didn't I?

Notes

'Yacchaimashita ne' is a casual spoken expression used when the speaker realizes that they have made a mistake or done something they should not have done. It is often close to a self-deprecating 'I messed up, didn't I?' rather than a neutral report of failure. 'Yacchau' is the colloquial form of 'yatte shimau,' and here it means that the speaker has messed up, not that they have achieved something. The final 'ne' makes it sound as if the speaker is also checking or sharing that realization with the listener. For success or achievement, Japanese normally uses 'yarimashita,' not 'yacchaimashita.' For example, 'Yamamoto, yarimashita' or 'Yamamoto senshu, yarimashita' can be used playfully, even outside sports, as if the speaker were celebrating their own success in a live broadcast.

2026.06.30 Tue / No. 811 / nebatta

It was worth sticking it out.

粘ねばったかいがあったよ。

Nebatta kai ga atta yo.

It was worth sticking it out.

Variants

  • 粘ねばったかいがあったよ。
    It was worth sticking it out.
  • ここまで粘ねばったかいがあったよ。
    It was worth sticking it out this far.
  • 頑張がんばったかいがあったよ。
    It was worth the effort.

Adjusted Expression

ここまで粘ねばったかいがありました。

It was worth sticking it out this far.

Example

A「とうとう完成かんせいしたね」 B「うん、ここまで粘ねばったかいがあったよ」

In the example, 'koko made nebatta kai ga atta yo' expresses the feeling that sticking it out this far was worth it because it finally led to completion.

2026.07.02 Thu / No. 813 / doushite

Why didn't you say that first?

どうして、それを先さきに言いってくれないの？

Doushite, sore o saki ni itte kurenai no?

Why didn't you say that first?

Variants

  • どうして、それを先さきに言いってくれないの？
    Why didn't you say that first?
  • どうして、そんなこと先さきに言いってくれないの？
    Why didn't you mention that earlier?
  • どうして、もっと早はやく言いってくれなかったの？
    Why didn't you tell me that sooner?

Adjusted Expression

どうして、それを先さきに言いってくれなかったのですか？

Why didn't you tell me that earlier?

Example

A「どうして、それを先さきに言いってくれないの？」 B「だって、誰だれも聞きかなかったから」

A: Why didn't you say that first? B: Because nobody asked me.

Notes

'Doushite, sore o saki ni itte kurenai no?' is an immediate spoken reaction when important information is revealed too late. It is not simply asking for a reason. It also conveys the feeling that things could have turned out differently if the information had been given earlier. 'Saki ni' shows that the speaker is reacting not just to the information itself, but to the timing of when it was given. 'Itte kurenai no?' carries a mild tone of complaint rather than strong criticism. In the example, 'dare mo kikanakatta kara' means 'because nobody asked me.' It is different from 'dare mo kiite kurenakatta kara,' which means 'because nobody listened to me.'

You could at least clean your own room.

部屋へやの掃除そうじぐらい、やってくれたっていいんじゃない。

Heya no souji gurai, yatte kureta tte ii n janai.

You could at least clean your own room.

Variants

  • 部屋へやの掃除そうじぐらい、やってくれたっていいんじゃない。
    You could at least clean your own room.
  • 自分じぶんの部屋へやぐらい、掃除そうじしてくれたっていいんじゃない。
    You could at least tidy up your own room.
  • 自分じぶんの部屋へやぐらい、片付かたづけてくれたっていいんじゃない。
    You could at least clean up after yourself.

Adjusted Expression

部屋へやの掃除そうじぐらいは、していただけませんか。

Could you at least clean your own room?

A「じゃ、あと、よろしく」 B「ちょっと待まって。自分じぶんの部屋へやの掃除そうじぐらい、やってくれたっていいんじゃない」

A: Okay, you can take care of the rest. B: Wait a minute. You could at least clean your own room.

Notes

'Heya no souji gurai, yatte kureta tte ii n janai' is a casual spoken expression used when the speaker feels that the other person should at least do something that is considered a minimum responsibility. 'Gurai' suggests that the task is not asking too much, and 'yatte kureta tte ii n janai' carries the feeling of 'You could at least do that.' Rather than giving a direct command, the speaker expresses mild dissatisfaction while implying that the responsibility naturally belongs to the listener.

2026.07.04 Sat / No. 815 / 180do

Things did a complete 180.

180度ど、変かわっちゃいました。

180-do, kawacchaimashita.

Things did a complete 180.

Variants

  • 180度ど、変かわっちゃいました。
    It changed 180 degrees.
  • 180度ども変かわっちゃいました。
    It changed completely.
  • 全然ぜんぜん違ちがうものに変かわっちゃいました。
    It turned into something completely different.

Adjusted Expression

状況じょうきょうが180度ども変かわってしまいました。

The situation has changed completely.

Example

A「なんか、方針ほうしんが180度ど、変かわっちゃいましたね」 B「しかたないよ、予算よさんがないんだから」

A: Somehow, the policy has changed 180 degrees. B: It can't be helped; we don't have the budget.

Notes

'180-do, kawacchaimashita' is a casual spoken expression used when a situation, policy, opinion, or other matter has changed so drastically that it seems to have gone in the opposite direction. '180 degrees' is used metaphorically to mean 'a complete reversal.' 'Kawacchaimashita' is the colloquial form of 'kawatte shimaimashita,' often conveying surprise or disappointment about the unexpected change. In the example, the speaker is reacting to a major change in policy.

2026.07.05 Sun / No. 816 / doumitemo

No matter how you look at it, it's clear that...

どう見みても、...

Dou mite mo, ...

No matter how you look at it, it's clear that...

Variants

  • どう見みても、...
    No matter how you look at it, ...
  • どのように見みても、...
    Whichever way you look at it, ...
  • どこから見みても、...
    Any way you look at it, ...

Adjusted Expression

どのように見みても、明あきらかに...

No matter how you look at it, it's clearly ...

Example

A「この計画けいかく、本当ほんとうに成功せいこうすると思おもう？」 B「いやぁ、どう見みても、失敗しっぱいするよ」

A: Do you really think this plan will succeed? B: No, no matter how you look at it, it's going to fail.

Notes

'Dou mite mo' is a casual spoken expression used when the speaker presents a judgment as obvious, without explicitly saying 'clearly' or 'obviously.' It is not limited to visual observation. It is also used when the speaker feels that the conclusion follows from the overall situation. In English, 'No matter how you look at it' uses a general 'you,' not necessarily the specific listener. It means that no matter who looks at the situation, or from what angle, the same conclusion follows. In the example, the speaker presents the failure of the plan not merely as a personal opinion, but as something that anyone would conclude.
