2025.02.05 Wed / No. 301 / tappuri

Plenty of

たっぷり

Tappuri

Plenty of

Variants

  • たっぷり
    plenty of
  • 時間がたっぷりある
    have plenty of time
  • たっぷり使える
    can use it generously

Adjusted Expression

今日きょうは、自由じゆうに使つかえる時間じかんが十分じゅうぶんにあるので、うれしいです。

I am happy because I have enough time today that I can use freely.

Example

今日きょうは、自由じゆうな時間じかんがたっぷりあってうれしい。

I'm happy because I have plenty of free time today.

Notes

"Tappuri" is a word used to express an abundant amount of something, such as time, space, or resources. It means "plenty of" or "a generous amount of." This expression is commonly used to indicate that something is available in a sufficient or generous amount. The difference between "tappuri" and "takusan" is that "tappuri" implies not just a large quantity, but also that the amount is sufficient and can be fully utilized.

"Ima no kikoeta?" is a phrase used to ask if someone heard a sound or noise that occurred just now. The word "ima" means "now," and "kikoeta" is the past tense of the verb "kikoeru" (to hear). Since "kikoeru" already expresses audibility, it does not normally form a separate potential form. Therefore, the negative form is "kikoenai."

Variants

  • 誰を待っているの？
    who are you waiting for?
  • 誰待ってるの？
    who are you waiting on?
  • 誰を待っていますか？
    whom are you waiting for?

Adjusted Expression

A「どなたをお待まちですか」B「友人ゆうじんを待まっています」

A: Who are you waiting for? B: I am waiting for a friend.

Example

A「誰だれを待まっているの？」B「友ともだち」

A: Who are you waiting for? B: My friend.

Notes

"Dare o matte iru no?" is a phrase used to ask who someone is waiting for. The word "dare" means "who," "o" is a particle that marks the object of the verb, "matte iru" is the progressive form of the verb "matsu" (to wait), and "no" is not possessive here but a soft sentence-final particle used when asking gently. In casual conversation, using "ka" after a plain form can sound blunt or rough, so "no" is often used instead. In more formal conversation, "desu ka" or "masu ka" is appropriate. The reason for using "o" in "dare o" is to distinguish the person being waited for from the person who is waiting. When the context makes the relation clear, particles may be omitted and the meaning can still be understood.
