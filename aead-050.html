So that's what you mean?

そういうこと？

sou iu koto?

So that's what you mean?

Variants

  • そういうことですか？
    So that's what you mean?
  • そういう意味いみですか？
    Is that what you mean?
  • そういうことなんですか？
    Is that what you're saying?

Adjusted Expression

それは、そういう意味いみですか？

Is that what you mean?

Example

A「この問題もんだいを解決かいけつするには、新あたらしい方法ほうほうが必要ひつようだと思おもう」 B「そういうこと？」 A「うん、今いまのやり方かたじゃ、時間じかんがかかりすぎるからね」

A: I think we need a new method to solve this problem. B: So that's what you mean? A: Yes, because the current way takes too much time.

Notes

"So that's what you mean?" is used when the speaker takes up the listener's previous statement and checks whether their understanding is correct. "Sou iu koto" receives the preceding statement as a whole and returns it to the listener as a summarized meaning. It does not simply ask about the meaning of a word. It checks the listener's intention or the main point of the explanation and helps organize the conversation. The short form "sou iu koto?" is casual, while "sou iu imi desu ka?" is more suitable in a formal situation.

Is that enough?

それで足たりる？

sore de tariru?

Is that enough?

Variants

  • それで足たりますか？
    Is that enough?
  • それで十分じゅうぶんですか？
    Is that sufficient?
  • それで大丈夫だいじょうぶですか？
    Will that be okay?

Adjusted Expression

それで足たりますか？

Is that enough?

Example

A「割わり勘かんでいい？」 B「うん。じゃ、これ、私わたしの分ぶん。それで足たりる？」 A「うん、ありがとう」

A: Is it okay to split the bill? B: Yes. Then this is my part. Is that enough? A: Yes, thank you.

Notes

"Is that enough?" is used to check whether the amount, money, information, or other item just offered is sufficient. "Sore de" refers to what has already been shown or offered, and "tariru?" asks whether there is any shortage. The expression does more than check quantity. It also suggests that the speaker is prepared to add more if it is not enough. It is useful in situations involving shared payment, money, materials, time, explanations, or anything where a required amount matters.

  • 今いまは、これが中心ちゅうしんなんですけど、...

Adjusted Expression

Example

Notes

"This is the main thing right now, but..." presents the activity or object that is currently central while leaving open the possibility that the situation may change. "Ima wa" limits the statement to the present, and "kore ga mein" identifies the current focus. By ending with "nan desu kedo," the speaker leaves room for future development, another plan, or additional explanation. The expression does more than state the current topic. It serves as an entry into an explanation such as "this is the main thing for now, but it may develop later."

"Shuumatsu wa souji shiteru" more clearly presents cleaning as part of how the speaker spends weekends, while "shuumatsu ni souji shiteru" more strongly marks the time when the cleaning is done.

独特どくとくな雰囲気ふんいきがあるね。

Dokutoku na fun'iki ga aru ne.

You have a unique vibe.

Variants

  • 独特な雰囲気があるね。
    You have a unique vibe.
  • 独特な感じがするね。
    You have a distinctive feel.
  • 独特なオーラがあるね。
    You have a unique aura.

Adjusted Expression

独特どくとくな雰囲気ふんいきを持もっていますね。

You have a distinctive atmosphere.

Example

A「この服ふく、似合にあう？」 B「うーん、独特どくとくな雰囲気ふんいきがあるね」

A: Does this outfit suit me? B: Hmm, you have a unique vibe.

Notes

'Dokutoku na fun'iki ga aru ne.' is used to comment on someone's distinctive impression or presence. It can work as a compliment, but it can also sound slightly ambiguous because the speaker is not clearly saying that something suits the person or is good. In the example, B does not directly say that the outfit suits A, but says that A has a unique vibe.
